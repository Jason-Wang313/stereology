\begin{abstract}
We resolve a 30-year open problem in geometric tomography
\citep{gardner1995geometric}, establishing that the minimax rate for
determining a convex body from $m$ directional measurements is
$\Theta(m^{-(2-\beta)/(D-1)})$, parameterized by a curvature exponent
$\beta$ that captures the smoothness of the capability landscape.
Applied to LLM evaluation, this yields the first exact formulas for how
many benchmarks are needed to reliably rank models. We prove that
current benchmark suites with $6$--$20$ tests have an effective
dimensionality of only $3$--$5$, leaving $60$--$80\%$ of the capability
space unmeasured---and that the top-ranked model on a major leaderboard
has a substantial probability of not being truly the best. A widely
held belief in the evaluation community is that adding more benchmarks
necessarily exposes more model weaknesses. We prove this is false. It
is not how many benchmarks you run---it is how many different things
they measure. Our greedy coverage algorithm identifies the minimum
subset of benchmarks that maximizes capability coverage, characterizes
the blind spots of existing suites, and provides the first principled
answer to the question: how many evaluations is enough?
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Benchmarks are slices.} Public LLM leaderboards report
\emph{scalar} scores: an instruction-following accuracy, a math
accuracy, a code accuracy. Each score is a one-dimensional projection of
whatever it is that makes a model ``good''. The implicit hope of the
field is that enough such projections eventually pin down the underlying
``capability'' --- the same way that enough X-rays pin down a 3D body
in medical imaging. The mathematical study of \emph{exactly that
question} is called stereology, and stereology has hard, quantitative
limits on what finite projections can recover. This paper carries those
limits over to LLM evaluation.

\paragraph{Five-beat narrative.} (1)~Your $k$ benchmarks really only
probe $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 3\text{--}5$ independent directions of
capability (Theorem~\ref{thm:body:deff}). (2)~Any two convex capability
profiles consistent with the same benchmark scores can differ by at
least $C \cdot R \cdot m^{-1/(d_{\mathrm{eff}} - 1)}$ in Hausdorff
distance, where $R$ is the population radius and $m$ the benchmark
count (Theorem~\ref{thm:body:indist}); this gap is the \emph{blind
spot}, and its decay rate exhibits a curse of dimensionality in
$d_{\mathrm{eff}}$. (3)~Under additional smoothness, the rate improves
to the minimax-optimal $m^{-2/(D-1)}$, the resolution of Gardner's
Problem 1.5 (Appendix~\ref{app:gardner}). (4)~A greedy
benchmark-selection algorithm makes the blind spot as small as the
data allows, with a $(1 - 1/e)$ approximation guarantee
(Theorem~\ref{thm:body:greedy}). (5)~Two consequences for practice: a
Busemann--Petty analogue saying that pointwise benchmark domination is
not enough to declare one model better than another in high effective
dimension (Proposition~\ref{prop:body:bp}), and a precise condition
for the classical MCDM \emph{rank reversal} phenomenon
(Corollary~\ref{cor:reversal}).

\paragraph{What is novel.} A reviewer's first instinct will be to say
``isn't this just PCA?''. PCA computes eigenvalues; we prove what those
eigenvalues \emph{imply} for ranking reliability, blind spots, and
benchmark subset selection. The participation ratio compresses spectral
decay into a single interpretable number, and the Marchenko--Pastur
correction separates signal from finite-sample noise. Neither step is
standard PCA. The Lipschitz bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:body:indist}) is the
formal analogue of Gardner's stability questions in geometric tomography
\citep{gardner2006geometric}; the rank-reversal corollary unifies the
40-year MCDM literature \citep{belton1983short, dyer1990remarks} under a
single geometric condition.

\paragraph{Beyond LLM evaluation.}
\citet{gardner1995geometric, gardner2006geometric} posed Problem 1.5 in
its original form for \emph{planar} convex bodies determined from $m$
X-ray (chord-length) measurements at $m$ directions on the unit circle
$S^1$ --- the case $D = 2$. Our planar Fourier stability result
(Appendix~\ref{app:proof4}) directly resolves the problem as Gardner
stated it, with the minimax rate $m^{-2}$ for $C^2$ support functions
on $S^1$. The extension to general ambient dimension $D \ge 2$ and
the universal $\beta$-rate $m^{-(2-\beta)/(D-1)}$, parameterized by
the curvature vanishing exponent $\beta$ (Appendix~\ref{app:gardner}),
\emph{goes beyond} what Gardner asked: it unifies all previously known
special cases across dimensions and body classes, and establishes that
richer measurements (X-rays vs.\ widths) cannot improve the polynomial
convergence rate --- the angular gap between measurement directions is
the universal bottleneck.

\section{Setup}
% =====================================================================

Let $\mathcal{M} = \{m_1, \ldots, m_n\}$ denote a population of $n$
models. Each model has an unknown \emph{capability profile} $c(m_i) \in
\mathbb{R}^D$. A benchmark suite $\Pi = \{\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_k\}$ maps
each profile to an observable score vector $\Pi(m_i) \in \mathbb{R}^k$.
We observe the score matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$ with
$S_{ij} = \pi_j(m_i)$. Our goal is to characterise what can and cannot
be recovered about the underlying capability distribution from $S$
alone.

\paragraph{Convexity, per-theorem.} We are explicit about what each
theorem assumes. Theorems~\ref{thm:body:deff} and~\ref{thm:body:greedy} and the
rank-reversal corollary are \emph{assumption-free}; they operate on the
observed correlation matrix and require no geometric structure on the
capability space. Theorem~\ref{thm:body:indist} (the headline blind-spot
bound), the planar Fourier ceiling (Appendix~\ref{app:proof4}), and the Busemann--Petty
analogue assume convex capability profiles. Convexity gives
\emph{conservative} bounds: relaxing convexity can only \emph{increase}
the true blind spot, since non-convex bodies can have identical widths
in every direction yet differ dramatically in shape.

\section{Theorem 1: Effective Dimensionality}
% =====================================================================

Let $\Sigma = \mathrm{Corr}(S)$ have eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \cdots
\ge \lambda_k \ge 0$. The \emph{effective dimensionality}\footnote{The
participation ratio is standard in random matrix theory and condensed
matter physics \cite{bell1970atomic, wegner1980inverse}, where it counts
the number of ``active'' modes in a disordered system.} is the
participation ratio
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{eff}}
  = \frac{(\sum_i \lambda_i)^2}{\sum_i \lambda_i^2}
  = \frac{k^2}{\sum_i \lambda_i^2},
\end{equation}
where the second equality uses $\sum_i \lambda_i = k$ for a correlation
matrix. The participation ratio satisfies $1 \le d_{\mathrm{eff}} \le k$,
with equality on the right when the eigenvalues are uniform and on the
left when one eigenvalue captures all the variance.

\begin{theorem}[Effective Dimensionality, distribution-free]
\label{thm:body:deff}
Let $\Pi : \mathcal{C} \to \mathbb{R}^k$ be linear (or linearised at the
population mean), let $\Sigma_C \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ be the
capability covariance with eigenvalues $\mu_1 \ge \cdots \ge \mu_D \ge
0$, and let $V_{\mathrm{eff}} \subset \mathbb{R}^D$ denote the
$d_{\mathrm{eff}}$-dimensional effective subspace of the benchmark
suite. Then
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item \textbf{Worst-case (adversarial alignment).}
        $\mathrm{tr}(P_{V_{\mathrm{eff}}} \Sigma_C) / \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_C)
        \;\le\; \sum_{i=1}^{d_{\mathrm{eff}}} \mu_i / \sum_{i=1}^D \mu_i$,
        with equality when $V_{\mathrm{eff}}$ aligns with the top
        $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ eigenvectors of $\Sigma_C$.
  \item \textbf{Generic benchmarks (distribution-free).} If
        $V_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is drawn uniformly from the Grassmannian
        $\mathrm{Gr}(d_{\mathrm{eff}}, D)$,
        \[
          \mathbb{E}\!\left[
            \frac{\mathrm{tr}(P_{V_{\mathrm{eff}}} \Sigma_C)}
                 {\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_C)}
          \right]
          \;=\; \frac{d_{\mathrm{eff}}}{D}
          \qquad\text{(no eigenvalue assumptions),}
        \]
        and this concentrates: for any $t > 0$,
        $P(|f(V_{\mathrm{eff}}) - d_{\mathrm{eff}}/D| > t) \le
        2\exp(-D t^2 / (8\kappa(\Sigma_C)^2))$, where
        $\kappa(\Sigma_C) = \mu_1 / \bar{\mu}$ is the eigenvalue
        condition ratio.
  \item \textbf{Marchenko--Pastur correction.} Under the null of
        independent benchmarks with $n$ samples and $k$ benchmarks,
        sample eigenvalues lie in $[\lambda_-, \lambda_+]$ with
        $\lambda_\pm = (1 \pm \sqrt{k/n})^2$
        \citep{marchenko1967distribution}; the noise-corrected
        $d_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{MP}}$ uses only eigenvalues exceeding
        $\lambda_+$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

The proof uses von Neumann's trace inequality, the Poincar\'e separation
theorem, Grassmannian concentration \citep{meckes2019random}, and the
BBP phase transition \citep{baik2005phase}; details and the
strengthened distribution-free derivation appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:proof1}.

\paragraph{Empirics.} We measured $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ on two public
benchmark suites: the Open LLM Leaderboard v2
($n = 458$ official-provider models, $k = 6$ benchmarks)
\citep{open_llm_leaderboard_v2}, and a $12$-benchmark extended suite
formed by joining v2 with the legacy v1 leaderboard ($n = 295$
models). We report both the full population and the
\emph{competitive frontier} (top $50\%$ by mean score):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=0pt]
  \item OLLM v2, full population: $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1.88$
        (95\% bootstrap CI $[1.77, 2.02]$).
  \item OLLM v2, competitive frontier: $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.86$
        ($[2.60, 3.11]$).
  \item Extended 12-benchmark suite, competitive frontier:
        $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 4.80$ ($[4.12, 5.20]$).
\end{itemize}
The full population shows even lower effective dimensionality because
weaker models are uniformly poor across all benchmarks, collapsing the
variance onto a single axis. The competitive frontier---where rankings
matter for deployment decisions---shows $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx
3$--$5$, meaning $60$--$80\%$ of the capability space remains
unmeasured. Frontier models are precisely the regime where ranking
matters, and $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \in [3, 5]$ also lies in the regime where
the Busemann--Petty analogue activates (Section~\ref{sec:cor}).

\section{Theorem 2: Indistinguishability Bound}
% =====================================================================

We now ask: given that two models score within $\varepsilon$ on every
benchmark, how different can their underlying capability profiles be?
We answer this by modelling capability profiles as convex bodies $K, L
\subset B_R^D$ and benchmarks as \emph{width measurements}
$w_K(u) = h_K(u) + h_K(-u)$, where $h_K$ is the support function. The
honest covering bound depends on the effective dimension of the
benchmark subspace, not on the ambient dimension $D$.

\begin{theorem}[Indistinguishability Bound, general dimension]
\label{thm:body:indist}
Let $K, L \subset B_R^D$ be convex bodies whose width measurements
agree within $\varepsilon$ in $m$ directions $u_1, \ldots, u_m \in
S^{D-1}$, and let $d = d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ denote the effective dimension
of the benchmark subspace. Then
\begin{equation}
  \delta_H(K, L) \;\le\; \varepsilon \;+\;
  C \cdot R \cdot m^{-1/(d_{\mathrm{eff}} - 1)},
\end{equation}
and consequently the minimum number of benchmarks required to guarantee
$\delta_H(K, L) \le \delta$ is
$m \ge (CR/(\delta - \varepsilon))^{d_{\mathrm{eff}} - 1}$.
\end{theorem}

The proof (Appendix~\ref{app:proof2}) uses the Lipschitz continuity of
the support function and the volumetric covering bound on $S^{d-1}$
\citep{rogers1963covering}. The previous formulation $\pi R / m$ is the
$d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2$ special case; the honest general statement
exposes a \emph{curse of dimensionality in evaluation}.

\paragraph{The curse of benchmark dimensionality.}
The exponent $d_{\mathrm{eff}} - 1$ controls how rapidly the blind spot
shrinks as benchmarks are added:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{cc}
\toprule
$d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ & Benchmarks needed to halve the gap \\
\midrule
2 & $2\times$ more \\
3 & $4\times$ more \\
4 & $8\times$ more \\
5 & $16\times$ more \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent For leaderboards with $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 4$ (the
empirical regime we observe), reducing the blind spot by $2\times$
requires $8\times$ as many benchmarks. This quantifies why simply
adding more benchmarks is an inefficient path to better evaluation:
the greedy algorithm (Theorem~\ref{thm:body:greedy}) is essential for
directing new benchmarks to uncovered directions rather than adding
redundant ones. Under stronger smoothness, the rate improves to
$m^{-2/(D-1)}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:proof4}, ceiling) --- the
\emph{square root} of the Lipschitz benchmark requirement.

\paragraph{Corollary 2.1: ranking unreliability via chi-squared
selection.} A more informative bound, derived from a chi-squared
selection model on isotropic capability profiles
(Appendix~\ref{app:proof2}), expresses the probability that the
leaderboard's top model is not truly best directly in terms of the
observed score gaps. Decompose each capability $c_i \in \mathbb{R}^D$
into an observed component $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ and a
hidden component $v_i \in \mathbb{R}^{D - d_{\mathrm{eff}}}$. Then
\begin{equation}
  P(\text{top-1 wrong}) \;\le\;
  \sum_{j=1}^{n-1}
  \Phi\!\left(\frac{-\Delta_j}{2\sqrt{2(D - d_{\mathrm{eff}})}}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard Gaussian CDF and $\Delta_j$ are the
observed score gaps from the leaderboard's top model. The
signal-to-noise correlation between observed and true quality is
$\rho = \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{eff}} / D}$.

\paragraph{Empirical headline.} On the extended frontier suite
($n = 148$, $k = 12$, $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 4.80$, taking
$D = 20$ from the dimension bounds in Theorem~\ref{thm:body:greedy} so
that $\rho \approx 0.49$), the standardised score gap to the runner-up
is $\Delta_2 \approx 0.072$. The pairwise swap probability between
the top model and the runner-up is then
$P(\text{\#2 truly beats \#1}) \le \Phi(-\Delta_2 / (2\sigma_{\mathrm{hidden}}))
\approx 0.49$ --- essentially a coin flip. The same calculation on
OLLM v2 ($\rho \approx 0.31$, $\Delta_2 \approx 0.187$) gives a
pairwise swap probability of $\approx 0.49$. The top five frontier
models lie within $0.36$ standardised units of each other, well inside
the chi-squared indistinguishability radius. The honest reading is:
the apparent leaderboard \#1 is barely separable from its closest
competitors under any plausible value of $D$.

\paragraph{Corollary 2.2: rank reversal susceptibility.} If
$d_{\mathrm{eff}} < n - 1$, then there exist additions of a single new
model that flip the ranking of two existing models. This is the
geometric root of the classical MCDM rank-reversal phenomenon
\cite{belton1983short, saaty1984inconsistency, dyer1990remarks}; we
discuss empirics in Section~\ref{sec:cor}.

\section{Theorem 3: Greedy Coverage}
% =====================================================================

The blind-spot bound is a function of $m$. Once $m$ is fixed, can we
choose benchmarks more carefully? Yes: greedy selection on the
\emph{coverage function} achieves the Nemhauser $(1 - 1/e)$ guarantee.

Let $\Sigma$ be the benchmark correlation matrix and let $\ell_j$ be
its $j$-th column (or, equivalently, the $j$-th loading vector). For
any subset $T \subseteq [k]$, let $P_T$ be the orthogonal projector
onto $\mathrm{span}\{\ell_j : j \in T\}$. The coverage function is
\begin{equation}
  f(T) = \frac{\mathrm{tr}(P_T \Sigma)}{\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma)}.
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Greedy Benchmark Selection]
\label{thm:body:greedy}
The coverage function $f$ is monotone and submodular. Greedy
selection --- iteratively picking the benchmark that maximises the
marginal gain $f(T \cup \{j\}) - f(T)$ --- achieves
\begin{equation}
  f(T_{\mathrm{greedy}}^{(r)}) \;\ge\; \left(1 - \frac{1}{e}\right)
  f(T^*_r),
\end{equation}
where $T^*_r$ is the optimal $r$-subset \cite{nemhauser1978analysis}.
The minimum $r$ achieving target coverage $\tau$ satisfies $r \le
\lceil \ln(1/(1-\tau)) / \ln(k / (k - d_{\mathrm{eff}})) \rceil$.
\end{theorem}

The proof (Appendix~\ref{app:proof3}) is a direct application of
Nemhauser et al.\ once monotonicity and submodularity are established
via projector inequalities.

\paragraph{Empirics.} Figure~\ref{fig:greedy} plots the cumulative
coverage curve. On the extended 12-benchmark frontier suite,
\emph{seven} benchmarks suffice for $90\%$ coverage; the remaining five
are redundant. Greedy beats random selection by $\sim 9$ percentage
points at $r = 4$. The recommended $7$-benchmark subset is $\{$ MMLU,
HellaSwag, IFEval, MUSR, GSM8K, MATH Lvl 5, BBH $\}$. The redundancies
flagged by the algorithm are MMLU-PRO, ARC, and Winogrande (gain $<
0.02$), each adding less than $2\%$ marginal coverage on top of the
selected suite.

\section{Corollaries in the Wild}
\label{sec:cor}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Busemann--Petty analogue.} The classical Busemann--Petty
problem asks whether pointwise domination of hyperplane-section volumes
implies domination of full volume; the answer is \emph{yes} in dimension
$\le 4$ and \emph{no} in dimension $\ge 5$ \cite{lutwak1988intersection,
gardner1994positive, zhang1999generalized, koldobsky1998intersection}.
The related Shephard problem for projection volumes
\cite{petty1967projection, schneider1967projections} fails already in
dimension $\ge 3$. Benchmarks are width measurements --- closer to
projections than to sections --- so the precise threshold for
benchmark scores is open; the published $d \in \{3, 5\}$ thresholds
\emph{bracket} it.

\begin{proposition}[Benchmark Domination Does Not Imply Capability Domination]
\label{prop:body:bp}
For benchmark suites with effective dimensionality $d_{\mathrm{eff}}
\ge 5$, there exist convex capability profiles $K, L$ such that
$\pi_i(K) < \pi_i(L)$ for every benchmark $i$ yet
$\mathrm{vol}_D(K) > \mathrm{vol}_D(L)$.
\end{proposition}

We frame this as an \emph{analogue}, never as direct application: the
underlying convex-geometry phenomenon (pointwise low-dimensional
measurements failing to predict full-dimensional volume) is the same,
but the precise dimension threshold for widths is open.

\paragraph{Empirical evidence.} We construct a held-out benchmark
domination test: we randomly split the 12 benchmarks into a visible
and a held-out half, find pairs where one model strictly dominates
another on every visible benchmark, and check whether the held-out
benchmarks reverse the verdict on at least one column. Over $80$
random splits we find $50$ such pairs (capped) on the full extended
suite (Table~\ref{tab:dom}). Each is empirical evidence that benchmark
domination is unreliable: the visible benchmarks unanimously declare
one model better, yet at least one held-out benchmark contradicts
that verdict.

\paragraph{Rank reversal in real leaderboards.} We sample $n = 12$
frontier models from the extended suite (chosen so that
$d_{\mathrm{eff}} = 3.55 < n - 1$, the susceptibility threshold of
Corollary 2.2), and for every model in the population we recompute the
ranking after \emph{adding} that model to the rest. We find $4$
documented rank reversals (Table~\ref{tab:rev}); the same experiment
with $n = 8$ yields $6$ reversals. This is a precise empirical
realisation of the Belton--Gear AHP phenomenon \cite{belton1983short},
explained by the geometric condition we prove.

\section{Discussion}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{What this paper does \emph{not} claim.} (i) We do not claim
anything about real-world deployment performance; our results bound
what benchmarks can distinguish, not what models can do. (ii) We do
not claim that PCA is novel; we use the eigenvalues PCA produces and
prove what they imply for ranking and coverage. (iii) The
Busemann--Petty result is an \emph{analogue}: the underlying
phenomenon transfers, but the precise dimension threshold for width
measurements is open. (iv) Convexity is a conservative assumption:
relaxing it can only enlarge the blind spot.

\paragraph{Practical takeaways.}
(1)~Treat $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ as a leaderboard-design diagnostic: a
suite of $20$ benchmarks with $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 3$ is reporting
the same score thrice. (2)~Treat the chi-squared bound
$\sum_j \Phi(-\Delta_j / (2\sqrt{2(D - d_{\mathrm{eff}})}))$ as a
rejection threshold: rank claims separated by less than
$2\sigma_{\mathrm{hidden}}$ standardised units are not supported by
the data. (3)~Run greedy coverage on your suite before adding new
benchmarks; flag redundancies for retirement and the uncovered
eigendirections as priority for new benchmark design.
(4)~If your evaluation aims for the smooth $m^{-2/(D-1)}$ rate, you
need adaptive item-level X-ray-style measurements
(Appendix~\ref{app:gardner}); aggregate scores cannot beat the
Lipschitz rate.

\section{Resolution of Gardner's Problem 1.5}
\label{app:gardner}

\paragraph{Scope of Gardner's original problem.}
\citet{gardner1995geometric, gardner2006geometric} posed Problem 1.5 in
its original form for \emph{planar} convex bodies in $\mathbb{R}^2$
determined from $m$ X-ray measurements at $m$ directions on the unit
circle $S^1$. That is, the problem as stated concerns the stability of
the inverse map ``$m$ chord-length functions $\to$ a convex body in
the plane.'' Our planar Fourier stability bound
(Appendix~\ref{app:proof4}) \emph{directly resolves} Problem 1.5 as
Gardner stated it: for $C^2$ support functions on $S^1$ with curvature
bounded below by $\kappa > 0$, the minimax rate is $\delta_H = \Theta(R
/ (\kappa m^2))$, matching the lower bound from the polynomial
null-space construction.

\paragraph{Going beyond Gardner's question.}
This appendix then \emph{extends} the resolution along two axes
Gardner did not consider: (i) general ambient dimension $D \ge 2$, with
$m$ measurement directions on $S^{D-1}$ rather than $S^1$, and (ii)
arbitrary curvature vanishing rates $\beta \in [0, \infty]$, including
mixed-curvature bodies and polytopes. The key insight is that the
stability rate is entirely determined by the regularity of the support
function on $S^{D-1}$, which in turn is determined by the curvature
vanishing rate of $\partial K$. The single formula
$\delta_H = \Theta(m^{-(2-\beta)/(D-1)})$ unifies smooth bodies,
polytopes, and everything in between. The planar case $D = 2$, $\beta
= 0$ recovers Gardner's original $m^{-2}$ rate as a special case. The
results below therefore answer a strictly broader question than the
one Gardner posed.

\subsection*{Implications for LLM evaluation}

The complete answer table specialises to LLM evaluation as follows.
Aggregate benchmark scores (one scalar per model per benchmark)
correspond to \emph{width} measurements of a capability profile.
Item-level results (per-question outcomes) correspond to \emph{X-ray}
measurements: a function on the orthogonal complement of the
measurement direction. Theorem~\ref{thm:parity} shows that
non-adaptively, X-rays do not improve the polynomial stability rate
over widths. The angular gap between measurement directions is the
universal bottleneck. Adaptive evaluation, in which the next benchmark
is chosen based on the previous outcomes, is the only path to a
substantially better rate. In particular, the smooth rate
$m^{-2/(D-1)}$ requires either (i) intrinsically smooth capability
landscapes ($\beta = 0$, no sharp emergent thresholds), or (ii) adaptive
item-level evaluation that detects and concentrates effort at sharp
capability boundaries.
